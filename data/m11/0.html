# MATH 1120 - Midterm II Product and quotient rule > $$ (fg)' = f'g + fg' $$ $$ \left( \frac{f}{g} \right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} $$
  • The first term has the derivative of the top.
  • This is similar to the product rule
  • The denominator is the square of the original denominator.
Chain rule > $$ (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x))g'(x) $$
Don't forget the final g'(x)
Limit of sine > $$ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 $$

The reciprocal also equals 1

Derivative of trig functions >
$$ \sin x \rightarrow \cos x $$ $$ \cos x \rightarrow -\sin x $$
$$ \tan x \rightarrow \sec^2 x $$ $$ \cot x \rightarrow -\csc^2 x $$
$$ \sec x \rightarrow \sec x \tan x $$ $$ \csc x \rightarrow -\csc x \cot x$$
  • Despite sin has the reciprocal csc, here it is on the same colume as sec
  • The derivative of the "opposite" functions are simply just a negative.
Derivative of inverse trig functions >
$$ \sin^{-1} x \rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt(1-x^2)} $$ $$ \cos^{-1} x \rightarrow -(\sin^{-1})' $$
$$ \tan x \rightarrow \frac{1}{1+x^2} $$ $$ \cot^{-1} x \rightarrow -(\tan^{-1})' $$
$$ \sec^{-1} x \rightarrow \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^2-1}} $$ $$ \csc^{-1} x \rightarrow -(\csc^{-1})' $$

The derivative of the "opposite" function is just the negative of the opposite function.

It is good idea to break down functions into sine and cosine, but answer in a combined function (for solving)
Exponential function > $$ (a^x)' = a^x \times \log x $$ Logarithmic > $$ (\log_b x)' = \frac{1}{x}\frac{1}{\ln b} $$
It is generally helpful to breakdown complex equation into many terms of long, as long as they don't contain product or power (can be removed using log rules).
